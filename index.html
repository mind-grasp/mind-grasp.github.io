Abstract

Occlusion poses a significant challenge to robotic grasping in cluttered scenes, especially when the target object is completely invisible. Under such full-occlusion conditions, the key difficulty shifts from grasp pose estimation alone to actively discovering the hidden target and ensuring a feasible retreat path. Existing methods mainly focus on partially visible targets, leaving target discovery and safe retreat under full occlusion insufficiently explored. To address this gap, we propose MIND-Grasp, a multi-view grasping framework inspired by human-like reasoning in occluded scenes. Given a language instruction and current observations, MIND-Grasp reconstructs an amodal 3D target model and the corresponding target-centric occlusion point cloud. Based on this occlusion cloud, MIND-Grasp searches for observable corridors, updates the target geometry in a closed loop, and executes grasp-remove only when critical occlusions must be removed. Once the target becomes visible and its geometry is reconstructed, the system further selects grasp poses that satisfy feasible-retreat constraints.

Method

Occluded Scene Reconstruction

Amodal Reconstruction and Observable Corridors

MIND-Grasp imagines amodal 3D target geometry under partial and full occlusion, then builds a target-centric occlusion cloud to guide closed-loop multi-view discovery through observable corridors.

Retraction-Aware Grasp Selection

Grasp Selection with Retreat Feasibility

MIND-Grasp plans an object-level retreat trajectory and selects a grasp whose gripper-target composite body can safely follow it. If no feasible grasp is found, the dominant blocker is removed and the retreat plan is updated.

Failure Cases and Extensions

Maintain multiple hidden-region beliefs when VLM priors are ambiguous, rather than relying on a single coarse target hypothesis.
Incorporate uncertainty-aware amodal 3D generation and closed-loop RGB-D correction to reduce geometry and pose errors in deep clutter.
Extend beyond parallel-gripper grasp-remove to pushing, pulling, dragging, opening, and dexterous hands for more human-like operable-space recovery.
